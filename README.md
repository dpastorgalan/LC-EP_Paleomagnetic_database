This repository contains a database with Late Carboniferous and Early Permian palepoles from Europe and North America.

## 1 Rationale
Following structure, petrology, geochronology and sedimentology a large collision involving Gondwana, Laurussia, and some other microplates happened during the Late Paleozoic. In Europe most geological arguments suggest the main collisional events end at around 330 or 320 million years ago (Ma hereafter) where Pangea was mostly formed. In North America the main collisional event seems to end slightly later, at about 310-300 Ma.

In contrast the Late Carboniferous and Early Permian paleomagnetic database of the world shows potential tectonic problems. Whereas the paleomagnetic database suggest large overlaps and important tectonic movements. The main pitfall is the scarcity of data. Here I have compiled a comprehensive database including paleomagnetic data from Europe and North America. This compilation is by no means a final project but will grow with time and new datasets. I am more than happy to add all datasets you know but I haven't included yet.

## 2 Selection criteria
I am considering the majority of poles for this compilation always that they contain an approximate age of magnetization. I do not collect data when the kinematics of vertical axis rotations are poorly constrained, either in magnitude or time. This is common in many places of the Mediterranean, like Corsica-Sardinia, Adria etc (e.g., Muttoni et al., 2009, Bachtadse et al., 2018, Edel et al., 2014 or Aubele et al., 2012). I am working to adapt those poles to a Pre-Triassic correct situation working with the newest plate reconstructions of the Mediterranean, I will add such poles as soon as the post-Permian rotations are clarified.

Paleomagnetism has evolved a lot since its golden years in the 80's and early 90's. Many of the preliminary papers published at that time show too few samples, not always properly demagnetized and in many cases analyzed with instruments that were not so sensitive. In my opinion it is already urgent the resampling of such areas to get newer poles, however, unfortunately most paleomagnetists are not interested in doing it. In this compilation I did not consider any site with less than 7 samples (like Wilkinson et al., 2017), which is the number of samples fromwhich *k* remains almost equal. I would love to be more selective and use the Deenen et al. criteria (2011), but right now it would reduce the database too much. I will be more selective as soon as new papers with new and high quality poles come out.

In many cases I have calculated a pole from several sites that were close together. Other times I have recalculated the age of remagnetization, always based on the newest available geochronological/structural data. Although I try to use as much as I can the original references, sometimes I use previous databases, I am sorry for that, little by little I will update those poles to give the correct reference.
